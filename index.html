A training philosophy

Train easy or train hard. Nothing in between.

Two intensities. That's all you need. Zone 2 builds the engine. Zone 5 raises the ceiling.

The principle

Most people train in the middle. It's the worst place to be.

Zone 3 and Zone 4 feel productive. They're hard enough to hurt, easy enough to sustain. But they don't trigger the adaptations that matter. Zone 2 builds your mitochondrial base. Zone 5 pushes your VO₂max. Spend your time where the returns are.

Running

Sixty minutes easy. Sixteen minutes hard. That's the week.

The aerobic base does the heavy lifting. High-intensity intervals provide the stimulus. Both are non-negotiable.

Zone 2

Build the base

Keep your heart rate at 60–70% of max for about 60 minutes. You should be able to hold a conversation, if only barely. If you can't finish a sentence, slow down.

For a lot of people, that means walking sections of the run. That's fine. The goal is time in zone, not pace. This is where you build mitochondrial density, improve fat oxidation, and lay the aerobic foundation everything else depends on.

If you have to breathe through your mouth, slow down.

Zone 5

The Norwegian 4×4

Developed by exercise physiologists at NTNU, the Norwegian 4×4 is one of the most evidence-backed protocols for increasing VO₂max — your body's ceiling for oxygen uptake and the single strongest predictor of cardiovascular health.

Warm-up: 10 minutes easy, 60–70% HRmax
4 intervals of 4 minutes at 90–95% HRmax, each followed by 3 minutes active recovery
Cool-down: 5 minutes easy

During the hard intervals, you should be uncomfortable but controlled. You need to sustain the effort for the full four minutes. Start with shorter intervals if you need to build up.

Strength

The deadlift

Glutes, hamstrings, lower back, traps, grip — the deadlift works the entire posterior chain in one movement. The same Z2/Z5 principle applies.

Zone 2

Light weight, perfect form

Pick a weight you can handle for 15+ reps with textbook form. Hinge at the hips, flat back, chest up, pull the slack out of the bar before you lift. Every rep should look identical.

This builds muscular endurance, reinforces motor patterns, and keeps your joints healthy. The goal is movement quality, not load.

The deadlift trains so many muscles that you'll hit temporary plateaus — grip strength lagging behind hip drive, for instance. Stay patient. The chain builds itself. The risk of bypassing a weak link is injury.

Zone 5

Heavy pulls, low reps

Raw strength work. Ramp up across 4–7 sets. The first two are warm-ups at moderate load, 5–8 reps. From set three onward, the bar should be heavy enough that 1–3 reps is all you can manage. Near-maximal pulls — controlled, braced, everything you've got.

Heavy deadlifts recruit maximal motor units, build bone density, strengthen connective tissue, and develop functional strength that transfers to everything else you do.
