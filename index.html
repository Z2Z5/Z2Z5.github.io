Z2Z5 strips fitness down to the two intensities that work: Zone 2 for building your aerobic engine, and Zone 5 for pushing your ceiling higher. Together they are amazing!

Running

Zone 2

Build the Base

Zone 2 means keeping your heart rate in the 60–70% of max range for an extended session — typically around 60 minutes. You should be able to have a conversation with some difficulty; if you can't hold a sentence, you're going too fast. For many people, this means walking portions of the run — and that's perfectly fine. The goal is time in zone, not speed.

Zone 2 training builds mitochondrial density, improves fat oxidation, and creates the aerobic foundation that everything else sits on.

Duration
~60 minutes
Heart Rate
60–70% of HRmax
Volume
Should be ~90% of your total training
Rule of Thumb
"If you have to breathe through your mouth, slow down."
Zone 5

The Norwegian 4×4

The Norwegian 4×4 interval protocol was developed by exercise physiologists at the Norwegian University of Science and Technology (NTNU). It is one of the most research-backed methods for increasing VO₂max — your body's maximum oxygen uptake and the single best predictor of cardiovascular health.

The protocol is simple:

  1. Warm-up: 10 minutes at an easy pace (60–70% HRmax / Zone 2)
  2. 4 intervals of 4 minutes at high intensity (90–95% HRmax), each followed by 3 minutes of active recovery (60–70% HRmax)
  3. Cool-down: 5 minutes easy

Total session time: ~35 minutes. During the hard intervals you should be uncomfortable but controlled and able to sustain the effort for the full 4 minutes.

Building up to it: start with fewer minutes at top speed. It's also normal that, to sustain all 4 intervals, you don't quite make it to Zone 5 in the first interval — that's OK.

Session Length
~35 minutes
Work Intervals
4 × 4 min at 90–95% HRmax
Recovery
3 min at 60–70% HRmax
Frequency
2–3 times per week
Modality
Running, cycling, rowing, or elliptical

Strength — Deadlift

The deadlift works the entire posterior chain — glutes, hamstrings, lower back, traps, grip — making it one of the most efficient full-body exercises. We use it for both Zone 2 and Zone 5.

Zone 2

Light Deadlifts, Perfect Form

In the Zone 2 approach, the goal is volume with light-to-moderate weight. Pick a weight you can handle for 15+ reps with textbook form.

The focus here is movement quality: hinge at the hips, flat back, chest up, pull the slack out of the bar before you lift. Every rep should look the same. This builds muscular endurance, reinforces motor patterns, and keeps joints healthy.

A note on patience: Since the deadlift works so many parts of the body, you may hit temporary plateaus that feel frustrating — like grip strength lagging behind everything else. Stick with the program. It builds the entire chain harmoniously. The risk of trying to bypass a weak link is injury.

Sets
4–7
Reps
15+ per set (may decrease as sets progress)
Weight
Light to moderate (form must never break down)
Rest
60–90 seconds between sets
Focus
Hinge pattern, flat back, controlled tempo
Zone 5

Heavy Deadlifts, Low Reps

This is where you build raw strength. The session structure is 4–7 sets, ramping up in weight. The first two sets serve as warm-ups at moderate intensity. From set 3 onward, the weight should be heavy enough that you can only manage 1–3 reps per set. These are near-maximal pulls — controlled, braced, all-out.

Heavy deadlifts recruit maximal motor units, build bone density, strengthen connective tissue, and develop the kind of functional strength that carries over to everything else.

Sets
4–7 total
Sets 1–2
Warm-up / moderate weight (5–8 reps)
Sets 3+
Heavy weight, 1–3 reps per set
Rest
3–5 minutes between heavy sets
Focus
Maximal effort, full bracing, controlled descent

Summary

Category Zone 2 (90% of volume) Zone 5 (5–10% of volume)
